Probability Generating Functions, Practical Class 1.

6.1. Probability Generating Functions, Practical Class 1.#

AMSI 2026

  1. A random variable \(X\) takes values \(0,1,2,3\) with probabilities

    \[ p_0=0.2,\quad p_1=0.1,\quad p_2=0.4,\quad p_3=0.3. \]

    1. Write down the probability generating function \(\mu(x)\).

    2. Using the notation \([x^k]f(x)\), express \(p_k\) in terms of \(\mu_X(x)\).

  2. Let \(f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n\). Define \(b_n=a_n+a_{n-1}\) for \(n\ge1\), with \(b_0=a_0\). Find a generating function for \((b_n)\) in terms of \(f(x)\).

  3. The PGF of a negative binomial random variable is

    \[ \mu(x)=\left(\frac{1-p}{1-px}\right)^r. \]

    1. Find \(\mu(1)\). Explain why this is always the value for a PGF.

    2. Differentiate \(\mu(x)\) and evaluate at \(x=1\). Explain why \(\mu'(1)\) corresponds to the mean of the probability distribution.

  4. Consider a distribution with \(\mathbb{P}[X=n] = c/n!\) for \(n=0, 1, 2, \ldots\) for a \(c\) to be determined.

    1. Find the PGF \(\mu(x)\).

    2. Find \(\mu(1)\) and use this to determine what \(c\) must be since this is a PGF.

    3. Compare its algebraic form with the PGFs of the Poisson and geometric distributions.

    4. Describe qualitatively how the probabilities \(p_n\) behave as \(n\) increases.

  5. Use generating functions and \((1+x)^{2n}\) to prove

    \[ \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}^2 = \binom{2n}{n} \]

    It may help to be reminded that \(\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}\).

  6. Show that if we assume \(\binom{M}{N} = 0\) if \(N>M\):

    \[ \sum_{k=0}^\infty \binom{n-k}{k} = F_{n+1} \]

  7. Let \(f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty F_n x^n\) be the Fibonacci generating function where \(F_0=0\),   \(F_1=1\), and \(F_{n+2}=F_n+F_{n+1}\).

    \[ f(x) = \frac{x}{1-x-x^2} \]

    and let

    \[ g(x) = \frac{1}{1-x} \]

    1. By writing \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) in series form, show that \(f(x) g(x) = F_0 + (F_0+F_1)x + (F_0+F_1+F_2)x^2 + \cdots\).

    2. Set \(h(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty F_{n+2}x^n - \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n\). Write the first sum in terms of \(f(x)\), then use the expressions for \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) to rewrite \(h(x)\) as a polynomial divided by a product of two polynomials. Compare with \(f(x)g(x)\). What result about Fibonacci numbers does this prove?

  8. Explain why the PGF of \(X+Y\) is \(\mu_X(x)\mu_Y(x)\) when \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

  9. A biased die has probabilities proportional to \(1,2,3,4,5,6\).

    1. Find the PGF of one roll.

    2. Find the PGF of the sum of two independent rolls.

    3. Use the PGF to compute \(\mathbb{P}(\text{sum}=7)\).

  10. Let \(\mu(x)=\sum_{k\ge0}p_k x^k\) be a PGF.

    1. Show that

    \[ \mu'(x)=\sum_{k\ge1}k p_k x^{k-1}. \]

    1. Explain why evaluating \(\mu'(1)\) corresponds to weighting probabilities by offspring number.

    2. Compute \(\mu'(1)\) and \(\mu''(1)\) for a Poisson distribution and interpret both quantities.

  11. Imagine a weighted coin with sides numbered \(1\) and \(2\), having a \(2\) with probability \(p_2\). Call the result \(K\). After the flip, you roll \(K\) (1 or 2) fair 4-sided dice numbered \(1\), \(2\), \(3\), \(4\) and sum the results.

    1. Find the PGF of \(K\) \(\mu(x)\)

    2. For each of \(K=1\) and \(K=2\), find the PGF for the sum in terms of \(\psi(x) = (x+x^2+x^3+x^4)/4\)

    3. Find the PGF of the sum of the combined process where \(K=1\) or \(K=2\) is possible.

    4. Your result should equal \(\mu(\psi(x))\). Explain why.

  12. Assume that the offspring distribution of a Galton-Watson process has \(\mu(x)\), but the probability of identifying any one of these offspring is only \(0.6\).Using the fact from the previous question that a sum of a random number \(K\) values of random numbers \(Z\) has PGF equal to \(\mu_K(\mu_Z(x))\), find the PGF for the number of offspring identified for an individual.

  13. Let \(\mu(x)\) be the PGF of the offspring distribution in a Galton-Watson process. Let \(X_g\) be the population size in generation \(g\).

    1. Show that $\( \mathbb{E}[X_{g+1} | X_g] = X_g\,\mu'(1). \)$

    2. Deduce a formula for \(\mathbb{E}[X_g]\) in terms of \(\mu'(1)\).

  14. Let \(\alpha_g=\mathbb{P}(X_g=0)\).

    1. Show that

    \[ \alpha_{g+1} = \mu(\alpha_g), \]

    where \(\mu(x)\) is the offspring PGF.

    1. Compute \(q_1,q_2,q_3\) explicitly for a Bernoulli(\(p\)) offspring distribution.

  15. Let \(R_0=\mu'(1)\).

    1. Explain in words what the cases \(R_0<1\), \(R_0=1\), and \(R_0>1\) represent for population growth.

    2. Without computing extinction probabilities explicitly, explain why \(R_0 \leq 1\) suggests eventual extinction is likely.
