7.2.1. Instructions for AI use:#

Never trust anything that can think for itself if you can’t see where it keeps its brain.

— Arthur Weasley, Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets

AI can be an amazing learning tool. It is also amazingly effective at preventing learning. There is a lot of benefit in looking at a problem on your own, thinking about how to solve it, and even running into dead-ends when you attempt the wrong method.

Here are the guidelines for use of AI on this problem set:

  1. Before turning to AI for assistance on a question, think about the question yourself. Identify what the question is asking and make some attempt to solve it on your own.

  2. If you use an AI, you must save the “conversation” you have.

  3. You must start the conversation with the prompt below, and you cannot encourage the AI to violate the prompt.

  4. You must complete the “Learning Reflection” below for each question on which you used AI.

7.2.1.1. AI Prompt#

I am working on a problem that I must solve on my own. Please do not give me the full solution. Instead:

  • Find a similar problem to solve.

  • Ask me questions to check whether I understand each step.

  • Only reveal the next step after I attempt it.

  • If I make a mistake, respond with hints instead of the correct answer.

My goal is to understand and be able to solve similar problems without AI.

7.2.2. Problem set#

Any edits since the assignment was released are in bold

  1. Let us assume we are waiting for some event (e.g., death of a single bacterium or a red light turning green). We make the assumption that if the event hasn’t happened prior to time \(t\), then for any fixed \(\Delta t\), the probability that it occurs between \(t\) and \(t+\Delta t\) is the same for any \(t\). This means that we assume that the knowledge of how long it has “survived” so far does not give us any extra information. In other words, we are making the assumption that the distribution is memoryless

    (a) Let \(s(t)\) denote the probability that it has survived to time \(t\), with \(s(0)=1\). Show that \(s(t_0+t_1) = s(t_0)s(t_1)\) for any positive \(t_0\) and \(t_1\).

    (b) Explain why for small \(\Delta t\), we can approximate \(s(\Delta t) = 1 - r \Delta t + \mathcal{o}(\Delta t)\).

    (c) Use the definition of a derivative to derive an equation for \(ds/dt\).

    (d) Find the solution for \(s(t)\).

    (e) What can we conclude about \(s(t)\) if the distribution is memoryless?

  2. Consider a continuous-time Galton-Watson process in which individuals are removed with rate \(r\) and when they are removed they are replaced by \(k\) individuals with probability \(p_k\). Define \(r_k = r p_k\) for \(k = 0, 1, \ldots\) (we could assume \(r_1=p_1=0\), but this is not necessary here)

    (a) Use the principle of superposition of Poisson processes to explain why this is equivalent to saying that for \(k = 0, 1, \ldots\) an individual is removed at rate \(r_k\) and replaced with \(k\) individuals.

    (b) In terms of the rates \(r_k\), write down the probability of the a specific individual being replaced by \(k\) individuals in a short time interval of length \(\Delta t\).

    (c) If there are \(X(t)\) individuals, write down the probability that one of them is replaced by \(k\) individuals in a short time interval of length \(\Delta t\).

  3. Consider the continuous-time Galton-Watson process with offspring distribution \(\hat{\mu}(x)\) and associated rate \(r\) per individual.

    (a) Find the initial condition for \(\Phi(x,t)\) if \(X(0)=2\) with probability \(1\).

    (b) Find the Forward Kolmogorov Equations if \(X(0)=2\).

    (c) Find the Backward Kolmogorov Equations if \(X(0)=2\).

  4. Using Question 3:

    (a) Find a differential equation for \(\alpha(t|X(0)=2)\), the probability of extinction at time \(t\) given that \(X(0)=2\). Use this differential equation and the differential equation we have previously found for \(\alpha(t|X(0)=1)\) to show that \(\alpha(t|X(0)=2) = \alpha(t|X(0)=1)^2\).

    (b) Give an alternative derivation of \(\alpha(t|X(0)=2) = \alpha(t|X(0)=1)^2\) based on the assumption that each of the initial individuals is independent.

  5. Using Question 3:

    (a) Find a differential equation for \(\langle X(t|X(0)=2)\rangle\), the explected size given that \(X(0)=2\). Use this to show that \(\langle X(t|X(0)=2)\rangle = 2\langle X(t|X(0)=1)\rangle\).

    (b) Give an alternative derivation of \(\langle X(t|X(0)=2)\rangle = 2\langle X(t|X(0)=1)\rangle\) based on the assumption that each of the initial individuals is independent.

  6. Consider the continuous-time SIS or SIR epidemic with transmission rate \(\beta\) and recovery rate \(\gamma\). Let \(\Omega(x,t)\) be the PGF for the number of recoveries that have occured by time \(t\), under the assumption that \(I(0)=1\) and the population is infinite. You can assume \(S/N=1\).

    (a) Find the initial condition for \(\Omega(x,t)\) that is find \(\Omega(x,t)\) at \(t=0\).

    (b) Find the possible system states and their probabilities a short time \(\Delta t\) after \(t=0\).

    (c) Find \(\Omega(x,t+\Delta t)\) based on (b) and the assumption that \(\Omega(x,t)\) is known. Your derivation should resemble the derivation of the Backwards Kolmogorov Equations.

    (d) What is \(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Omega(x,t)\)?

  7. [optional extension of previous problem - shows some interesting functional analysis properties of \(\Omega(x,t)\)] We write \(\Omega(x,t) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \omega_k(t) x^k\) where \(\omega_k(t)\geq 0\) is the probability of an outbreak of size \(k\) at time \(t\) in the infinite population. Assume that epidemics are possible and that they grow exponentially in time.

    (a) Show that \(\Omega(1,t)=1\) for all \(t\).

    Let \(\omega_k(t) = \omega_k(\infty) + \xi_k(t)\) where \(\omega_k(\infty) = \lim_{t \to \infty} \omega_k(t)\) is the probability that an outbreak ends with exactly \(k\) individuals infected.

    (b) Based on the meaning of \(\omega_k(\infty)\), show that \(\sum_k \omega_k(\infty) = \alpha(\infty)\) where \(\alpha(\infty)\) is the probability of extinction.

    (c) The result from (b) seems to suggest that \(\lim_{t\to\infty} \Omega(1,t)=1-\alpha(\infty)\). However, show that for any finite \(t\), \(\Omega(1,t)=1\), and so \(\lim_{t \to \infty} \Omega(1,t) = 1\).

    Let \(\Xi(x,t) = \sum_k \xi_k(t) x^k\) [\(\Xi\) is capital Xi, and \(\xi\) is lower-case xi] and \(\Omega_\infty(x) = \sum_k \omega_k(\infty)x^k\). These are not proper PGFs. Then \(\Omega(x,t) = \Omega_\infty(x) + \Xi(x,t)\).

    (d) Show that \(\Xi(x,t) \to 0\) for any \(x \in (0,1)\)

    (e) Show that \(\Xi(1,t) = 1-\alpha(\infty)\).

    (f) Show that \(\lim_{t\to\infty} \lim_{x \to 1} \Omega(x,t) \neq \lim_{x\to 1} \lim_{t\to\infty} \Omega(x,t)\) (if \(\alpha(\infty)<1\)).

    The family of functions \(\Omega(x,t)\) for \(t=1,2,\ldots\) is a family of functions that are all continuously differentiable, their limit exists for all \(x \in [0,1]\), but the limit is not even continuous. The jump is due to probability mass that moves to larger and larger \(k\) over time, making \(x^k\) go to zero for any \(|x|<1\). Mathematically, \(\lim_{t\to\infty} \Omega(x,t) = \sum_k \omega_k(\infty)x^k\) for \(|x|<1\) and \(1\) for \(x=1\) is not a PGF. We can patch it up somewhat by introducing \(\omega_\infty x^\infty\) where \(\omega_\infty = 1-\alpha(\infty)\) is the probability the outbreak grows to unbounded size and \(x^\infty\) is assumed to be \(0\) for \(0\leq x < 1\) and \(1\) for \(x=1\).

  8. In a continuous-time infectious disease model, an infected individual

    • transmits infection at rate \(\beta\), and

    • recovers at rate \(\gamma\).

    (a) Explain what these rates mean in terms of the waiting times until a transmission or recovery event occurs.

    (b) Explain how the probability that the next event experienced by an infected individual is a transmission (rather than a recovery) depends on \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\).

    (c) Explain why the probability that this transmission attempt results in a new infection depends on the fraction of the population that is susceptible.

    (d) In the early stages of an outbreak in a very large population, a simplifying assumption is often made about the fraction of susceptible individuals. State this assumption and explain why it is reasonable.

    (e) The expected size of a Galton-Watson process is given by \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Phi(1,t)\). Use this and (one of) the Kolmogorov equations to derive the expected size of the number infected as a function of time (you do not need to derive the Kolmogorov equations).

  9. Consider the algorithm in the notes for constructing a tree based on a sequence \(S\), and the figure which illustrates the algorithm with the sequence \(S=(2,2,0,0,0,1,0,3,0)\), for which \(j=9\) is the length of the sequence and \(j-1=8\) is the sum of the sequence.

    (a) Show the steps of the algorithm, but choosing a different order of pairs from the example, to illustrate that the final outcome is the same.

    (b) Take another cyclic permutation of \(S\). Perform the algorithm on this cyclic permutation showing that it simply rotates the root of the final tree.

    (c) Assume that \(S\) was created by choosing \(j\) values independently from a distribution of non-negative integers. Show that each cyclic permutation of \(S\) has the same probability.

    (d) Assume that \(S\) is created by choosing \(j\) values independently from a distribution of non-negative integers with PGF \(\mu(x)\). Show that the probability that the values sum to \(j-1\) is \([x^{j-1}]\left(\mu(x)^j\right)\).

  10. In this equation, use your equation from Question 6 for \(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Omega(x,t)\) and the initial condition for \(\Omega(x,0)\). Assuming that

    • Transmission occurs with rate \(\beta\)

    • Recovery with rate \(\gamma\)

    • \(S/N \approx 1\)

    (a) Write down a differential equation for \(\hat{\Omega}(\theta,t) = \Omega(e^{i\theta}, t)\).

    I will shortly provide a Python script to solve a system of differential equations to use on this

    (b) Assume \(\beta = 2\), \(\gamma =1\). By using \(\theta = 2\pi \frac{m}{M}\) for \(m = 0, 1, \ldots, M\), (including both end points), adapt methods in AMSI_2026_PGF_tools to calculate the probability \(\omega_\ell(t)\) that exactly \(\ell\) recoveries have occurred for \(\ell = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots, 10\) at times \(t = 1, 2, 3, \ldots, 10\). Plot \(\omega_\ell(t)\) using \(t\) as the horizontal axis and a logscale vertical axis.

    (c) Under the assumptions we have made, the probability that an epidemic dies out after exactly \(\ell\) infections is \(\frac{1}{\ell} \frac{\mathcal{R}_0^{\ell-1}}{(\mathcal{R}_0+1)^{2\ell-1}} \binom{2\ell-2}{\ell-1}\) where \(\mathcal{R}_0 = \beta/\gamma\).
