1.7. Size Distribution at successive times#

Please read Section 3.4, Section 3.5, and Section 3.6 before reading this section.

The major result we rely on in thiss section is that if \(\mu_1(x)\) and \(\mu_2(x)\) are both PGFs, then \(\psi(x) = \mu_1(\mu_2(x))\) is the PGF for the following process: First we choose \(k\) from the distribution of \(\mu_1\). Then we choose \(k\) numbers from the distribution of \(\mu_2\) and add them together.

This can be used to prove the following theorem:

Theorem 1.3 (Galton-Watson sizes at later generations)

If \(\mu(x)\) is the PGF of the offspring distribution of a Galton-Watson process, then the distribution of sizes at generation \(g\) has PGF \(\mu^{(g)}(x)\) where \(\mu^{(0)}(x) = x\) and \(\mu^{(g)}(x) = \mu^{(g-1)}(\mu(x)) = \mu(\mu^{(g-1)}(x))\).

We will give two proofs of this theorem. At first glance the difference between the two proofs appears trivial. However, later when we will look at continuous-time variations of the Galton-Watson process the two methods will lead to very different equations.

Proof. Forward Proof

We use induction on the generation \(g\). At the base case, \(g=0\), we know that \(X_0=1\), so \(\mu^{(0)}(x)=x\).

For the inductive step, we know that the distribution of \(X_{g-1}\) is \(\mu^{(g-1)}(x)\). By the definition of the Galton-Watson process, \(X_g\) is found by choosing \(X_{g-1}\) numbers from the offspring distribution and adding them together. So the PGF of the distribution of \(X_g\) is \(\mu^{(g-1)}(\mu(x))\).

We now show an equivalent proof:

Proof. Backward Proof

We again use induction on the generation \(g\). At the base case, \(g=0\), we know that \(X_0=1\), so \(\mu^{(0)}(x)=x\).

For the inductive step, we know that the distribution of \(X_{g-1}\) is \(\mu^{(g-1)}(x)\). We know that chain of descendents from each individual in generation \(1\) can be thought of as the start of its own Galton-Watson process, and the number of individuals at generation \(g-1\) from each of the generation \(1\) individuals is \(\mu^{(g-1)}(x)\).

Since the number of individuals at generation \(1\) has PGF \(\mu(x)\) and the number of descendents after \(g-1\) from each of these has PGF \(\mu^{(g-1)}(x)\), we conclude that the size distribution at generation \(g\) has PGF \(\mu^{(g)}(x)\).

1.7.1. Expected size of a Galton-Watson Process at generation \(g\)#

We have seen that the size of a Galton Watson process at generation \(g\), \(X_g\) has PGF \(\mu^{(g)}(x) = \mu(\mu^{(g-1)}(x))\). The average size at generation \(g\), \(\mathbb{E}[X_g]\) is given for \(g>1\) by

\[\begin{align*} \mathbb{E}[X_g] &= \left [\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \mu^{(g)}(x) \right|_{x=1}\\ &= \left [\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \mu(\mu^{(g-1)}(x)) \right|_{x=1}\\ &= \left[ \mu'(\mu^{(g-1)}(x)) \frac{\partial }{\partial x} \mu^{(g-1)}(x)\right|_{x=1}\\ &= \mu'(\mu^{(g)}(1)) \left[\mu^{(g-1)}(x)\right|_{x=1}\\ &= \mu'(1) \left[\mu^{(g-1)}(x)\right|_{x=1}\\ &= \mu'(1) \mathbb{E}[X_{g-1}] \end{align*}\]

The fact that \(\mathbb{E}[X_g]\) can be written in terms of \(\mathbb{E}[X_{g-1}]\) strongly suggests an inductive proof of the following theorem is possible:

Theorem 1.4 (Average Size of a Galton Watson Process after \(g\) generations)

The expected size of a Galton Watson process at generation \(g\) is

\[ \mathbb{E}[X_g] = [\mu'(1)]^g \]

The proof is given as an exercise.

1.7.2. Self-test#

  1. Use Theorem 1.3 to show that the probability of extinction after \(g\) generations is \(\mu^{(g)}(0)\).

  2. Use proof by induction to show that the expected size of generation \(g\) is \([\mu'(1)]^g\).

  3. Using the two previous answers, what is the expected size of Galton-Watson processes that are not extinct by generation \(g\)?
